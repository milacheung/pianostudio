# EXAMINER FEEDBACK SUMMARY (FICTIONAL SAMPLE)
# Compiled from examination session feedback, Meridian Music Board.
# All observations are fictional and for demonstration purposes only.

## Grade 1 — Common Examiner Observations

### What examiners reward at Grade 1
- Steady pulse maintained throughout pieces — even at slow tempos, consistency scores well
- Confident entries — students who begin without hesitation after preparation time receive positive examiner notes
- Finger legato that does not rely on the sustain pedal (Grade 1 candidates should not use pedal)

### Common weaknesses that cost marks at Grade 1
- Sight-reading: Most Grade 1 failures in sight-reading occur because candidates stop to correct mistakes rather than continuing. Examiners frequently note: "Student stopped three times to restart bar 2." Marks are deducted for each complete stop.
- Scales: Uneven finger weight in the left hand, particularly on the weak 4th and 5th fingers. This is the most noted technical issue at Grade 1 across all examination sessions.
- Aural: Students who clap the strong beat instead of the underlying pulse fail this component. Practice should focus on clapping the smallest note value rather than the felt beat.

## Grade 2 — Common Examiner Observations

### What examiners reward at Grade 2
- Aural tests: Students who sing back a phrase with musical expression (dynamics, shape) receive higher marks than those who sing it accurately but flatly. Musicality in aural work is specifically noted by examiners.
- Scales: Hands-together scales with balanced sound between hands. The left hand should not disappear under the right hand's melody in the scales component.
- Pieces: Clear articulation contrast between legato and staccato passages — examiners respond positively to students who commit to the marked articulations.

### Common weaknesses that cost marks at Grade 2
- Grade 2 candidates most commonly lose marks in the sight-reading component due to incorrect rhythmic values in dotted-note patterns. This is the single most frequently noted weakness across the entire Grade 2 examination session.
- Left-hand accompaniment patterns (Alberti bass, waltz patterns) that are uneven or louder than the melody. The melody must project above the accompaniment at all times; this is an explicit marking criterion.
- Aural: Students cannot identify incomplete (imperfect) cadences — they identify all cadences as "complete." Teachers should drill imperfect cadence recognition as a specific and separate exercise.

## Grade 3 — Common Examiner Observations

### What examiners reward at Grade 3
- Rubato that is controlled and returns to the pulse — examiners specifically distinguish between musical flexibility and poor tempo control.
- Scale technique: chromatic scales that are smooth and even receive specific commendation in examiner notes at this level.
- Sight-reading: Students who maintain a steady pulse through errors consistently score better than students who stop to correct.

### Common weaknesses that cost marks at Grade 3
- Grade 3 candidates most frequently lose marks on aural tests for the "mood and dynamics" description task. Students describe mood adequately but fail to name specific dynamic markings (forte, piano, crescendo). Teachers must teach the Italian terminology alongside the ear training from the beginning of Grade 3 preparation.
- Scales: The transition from one-octave scales to one-octave arpeggios causes memory errors in approximately 40% of Grade 3 candidates. Arpeggios should be drilled as a completely separate exercise from scales, not combined in the same practice segment.
- Pieces: Rubato that does not return to the underlying pulse is the most common reason for marks being deducted from List B (Romantic) pieces at Grade 3. Examiners note: "Phrasing was musical but the overall tempo was unclear by the final section." This can be corrected by practicing with a metronome at the phrase endings specifically.
